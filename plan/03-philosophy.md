# Philosophy Track — 1 hr/evening, ~17 weeks core

A practical, non-completionist path from the classical foundations to the philosophy that AI safety actually uses in 2026. Primary texts where they're load-bearing; excerpts, [SEP](https://plato.stanford.edu) articles, and podcasts everywhere else.

**Design principle — read the destination first.** Week 1 surveys where the field is *now*, so that every classic afterward is read *for* something: Hume for the orthogonality thesis, Aristotle for Claude's character training, Bentham for the moral circle that now reaches AI welfare, Parfit for what copying a digital mind does to personal identity.

**What got cut and why:** Sidgwick, Moore, Rawls, Descartes, Nietzsche, and most of Parfit are not load-bearing for this chain at 1 hr/day — SEP replacements are flagged inline. The single biggest recent shift: **AI moral status/welfare and constitution/character design have overtaken decision theory** as the areas frontier labs hire for and publish in ([Anthropic's model welfare program](https://www.anthropic.com/research/exploring-model-welfare), [Eleos AI](https://eleosai.org/research/), [NYU Center for Mind, Ethics, and Policy](https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/mindethicspolicy)).

**A second lens — flourishing, not just safety (weeks 14–16).** The safety canon's threat model is *loss of control*; the [Cosmos Institute](https://www.cosmos-institute.org/) (Brendan McCord; fellows include Jack Clark, Tyler Cowen, Philipp Koralus's [Oxford Human-Centered AI Lab](https://hailab.ox.ac.uk/)) works the complementary threat model: *loss of autonomy under well-functioning AI* — Tocquevillean soft despotism, cognitive atrophy via deference, centralized homogenization of thought. Their axis is political philosophy and social epistemology (Tocqueville, Mill's *On Liberty*, Hayek, Humboldt, Polanyi), which the analytic-ethics core below doesn't touch. Weeks 14–16 add that module, built largely from their [autonomy reading list](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/reading-list-ai-and-the-future-of) and [Oxford AI x Philosophy seminar](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/2025-oxford-seminar-ai-x-philosophy). One caveat: Cosmos is institutionally anti-pause and underweights catastrophic risk — treat it as a complement to weeks 1–13, not a substitute.

**Free editions:** [Early Modern Texts](https://www.earlymoderntexts.com) (Bennett translations of Hume, Kant, Bentham, Mill), [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org), [MIT Classics](http://classics.mit.edu), [utilitarianism.net](https://www.utilitarianism.net/), and open-access books noted below. Parfit, *Human Compatible*, and *The Precipice* are library items.

## Week 1 — Orientation: the destination

- 80,000 Hours, [Understanding the moral status of digital minds](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/moral-status-digital-minds/) (2 evenings)
- D'Alessandro, [Artificial Intelligence: Approaches to Safety](https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/phc3.70039) — the current survey of philosophy's contributions to AI safety (2)
- Hendrycks, [AI Safety, Ethics, and Society](https://www.aisafetybook.com/textbook) — machine ethics & [moral uncertainty](https://www.aisafetybook.com/textbook/moral-uncertainty) sections (2)

## Week 2 — The Greeks

- Plato, [*Euthyphro*](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1642) — "who decides the values?" is the constitution-design question, 2400 years early (2)
- Aristotle, [*Nicomachean Ethics* Books I–II](http://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.html) — virtue as trained disposition; the frame behind character training for LLMs (3)
- SEP, [Aristotle's Ethics](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle-ethics/) (1) · *Podcast: Philosophize This #005–006*

## Week 3 — Hume: the is–ought gap

- *Treatise* II.iii.3 ("reason is… slave of the passions") and III.i.1 (is–ought), [Bennett translation](https://www.earlymoderntexts.com/authors/hume) (3)
- SEP, [Hume's Moral Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hume-moral/) (2)
- Payoff: re-read the orthogonality section of Bostrom's [The Superintelligent Will](https://nickbostrom.com/superintelligentwill.pdf) — intelligence and goals are orthogonal *because* Hume was right (1) · *Podcast: Philosophize This #051–053*

## Week 4 — The utilitarians

- Bentham, [*Principles*](https://www.earlymoderntexts.com/authors/bentham) ch. 1, 4, and the ch. 17 footnote — "Can they *suffer*?" founds the moral circle that now reaches AI (2)
- Mill, [*Utilitarianism*](https://www.utilitarianism.net/) complete; chs. 2 and 4 slowly (4) · *Podcast: [BBC In Our Time — Utilitarianism](https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b05xhwqf)*

## Week 5 — Kant

- [*Groundwork*, Sections I–II](https://www.earlymoderntexts.com/authors/kant) — humanity as an end in itself; the ancestor of hard constraints in AI constitutions (4)
- SEP, [Kant's Moral Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant-moral/) §1–9 (2) · *Podcast: Philosophize This #056–059*

## Week 6 — Metaethics

- SEP, [Metaethics](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/metaethics/) (2)
- Mackie, *Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong*, ch. 1 — error theory, the default metaethic of much alignment thought (2)
- SEP, [Moral Anti-Realism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/moral-anti-realism/) + [Moral Non-Naturalism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/moral-non-naturalism/) (2)

## Week 7 — The moral circle & personal identity

- Singer, "All Animals Are Equal" (*Practical Ethics* chs. 2–3) (1)
- Parfit, *Reasons and Persons* chs. 10–13 — personal identity; what copying/merging does to a digital mind. Library item; substitute SEP [Personal Identity](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-personal/) + [Fin Moorhouse's Parfit explainers](https://finmoorhouse.com/writing/repugnant-conclusion/) (5)

## Week 8 — Population ethics & moral uncertainty

- Parfit chs. 16–17, 19, or SEP [The Repugnant Conclusion](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/repugnant-conclusion/) + [The Nonidentity Problem](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/nonidentity-problem/) (3)
- MacAskill/Bykvist/Ord, [*Moral Uncertainty*](https://www.williammacaskill.com/) chs. 1–2 (free PDF) — how to act when you don't know which ethics is true; directly relevant to what values we give AI (3) · *Podcasts: [FLI — MacAskill on moral uncertainty](https://futureoflife.org/podcast/moral-uncertainty-and-the-path-to-ai-alignment-with-william-macaskill/), 80k #17, #72*
- **Debate pair (flag for week 16):** if moral uncertainty argues for deferring to a wiser AI, Cosmos's [Live by the Claude, Die by the Claude](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-claude-boys) argues self-directed choice is *constitutive* of flourishing — deference can be correct and still corrosive.

## Week 9 — Philosophy of mind I: the classics

- Turing, [Computing Machinery and Intelligence](https://phil415.pbworks.com/f/TuringComputing.pdf) (1950) (1)
- Nagel, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1)
- Searle, "Minds, Brains, and Programs" + SEP [The Chinese Room Argument](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/chinese-room/) (2)
- Chalmers, [Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness](https://consc.net/papers/facing.pdf) + SEP [Functionalism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/functionalism/) (2) · *Podcast: 80k #67 (Chalmers)*

## Week 10 — Philosophy of mind II: LLMs

- Chalmers, [Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07103) (2)

- Perez & Long, [Evaluating AI Systems for Moral Status Using Self-Reports](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.08576) (1)
- Lindsey, [Emergent Introspective Awareness in LLMs](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection/index.html) — where this meets mech interp (1)

## Week 11 — The core safety arguments

- Bostrom, [The Superintelligent Will](https://nickbostrom.com/superintelligentwill.pdf) (1)

## Week 12 — Getting values into machines

- Yudkowsky, [Coherent Extrapolated Volition](https://intelligence.org/files/CEV.pdf) (1)
- Gabriel, [Artificial Intelligence, Values, and Alignment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.09768) (2)
- [Constitutional AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.08073) §1–2 (1)
- [Claude's Character](https://www.anthropic.com/research/claude-character) (1)
- [Claude's Constitution](https://www.anthropic.com/constitution) — read against Aristotle, Kant, and Mill; this is where the whole sequence converges (1+)
- **Debate pairs (flag for week 16):** McCord's [Raising Claude, Forgetting Us](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/raising-claude-forgetting-us) — careful formation of *Claude's* character, silence on Claude's formative effect on *users*; and Seth Lazar's [The Construction of Moral Character](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-construction-of-moral-character) — empirical evidence that LLM local moral competence is largely solved while coherence and rule-authority are not.

## Week 13 — AI welfare: the synthesis

- Long, Sebo et al., [Taking AI Welfare Seriously](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.00986) (2)
- Birch, [*The Edge of Sentience*](https://academic.oup.com/book/57949) (free, open access) — intro + precautionary framework + AI chapters (3)
- Eleos, [Key Concepts and Current Views on AI Welfare](https://eleosai.org/papers/20250127_Key_Concepts_and_Current_Views_on_AI_Welfare.pdf) (1) · *Podcasts: 80k #173 (Sebo), #196 (Birch), [Robert Long, Nov 2025](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/robert-long-eleos-ai-welfare-research/)*

## Week 14 — Autonomy: the classical core *(Cosmos module)*

The most-cited texts in the Cosmos corpus — all excerpt-length, all free.

- Tocqueville, [*Democracy in America*](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/816) Vol. II, Part 4, Ch. 6 — "soft despotism": a tutelary power that "hinders, restrains, enervates"; the sharpest pre-statement of the AI-convenience problem (1)
- Kant, [What Is Enlightenment?](https://www.columbia.edu/acis/ets/CCREAD/etscc/kant.html) — self-incurred immaturity; ~7 pages (0.5)
- Mill, [*On Liberty*](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/34901) chs. 2–3 — truth-seeking through disagreement; individuality as a component of well-being (2)
- Humboldt, [*The Limits of State Action*](https://oll.libertyfund.org/titles/humboldt-the-limits-of-state-action) ch. 2 — the germ of Mill's ch. 3 (1)
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* Book III chs. 1–5 — voluntary action and choice; extends week 2 (1)
- Synthesis: Cosmos, [In Defense of Self-Direction](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/in-defense-of-self-direction) — agency (executing choices) vs. autonomy (authoring a life) (0.5)

## Week 15 — Knowledge, decentralization & AI in public life *(Cosmos module)*

- Hayek, [The Use of Knowledge in Society](https://www.econlib.org/library/Essays/hykKnw.html) — why centralized optimizers can't aggregate dispersed knowledge; the standing argument against monolithic alignment (1)
- Polanyi, [The Republic of Science](https://www.missouriwestern.edu/orgs/polanyi/mp-repsc.htm) — science as spontaneous order (1)
- Adam Smith, *Theory of Moral Sentiments* VI.ii — the ["man of system"](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/58559) and the chessboard passage; pairs with Cosmos's [The Division of Judgment](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-division-of-judgment) on outsourcing judgment to agents (1)
- Tessler, Summerfield et al., "AI Can Help Humans Find Common Ground" (*Science* 2024, the "Habermas Machine") + [How Will Advanced AI Systems Impact Democracy?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.06729) (2)
- Nissenbaum, [contextual integrity](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/) — privacy as appropriate information flow (1)

## Week 16 — The philosopher-builder synthesis *(Cosmos module)*

Now run the debates flagged in weeks 8 and 12, plus the applied papers:

- Cosmos, [The Philosopher-Builder](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-philosopher-builder) + [Greatness and the Machine](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/greatness-and-the-machine) — productive friction as a design principle (1)
- [Live by the Claude, Die by the Claude](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-claude-boys) vs. week 8's moral uncertainty; [Raising Claude, Forgetting Us](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/raising-claude-forgetting-us) vs. week 12's Claude's Constitution — write up where you land (2)
- Koralus, [The Philosophic Turn for AI Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.18601) — autonomy-preserving agent design, from the Oxford HAI Lab (1)
- Sarkar, [AI Should Challenge, Not Obey](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.02263) (0.5)
- Stray et al., [What Are You Optimizing For?](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.10939) — recommender alignment as the precedent case (0.5)
- Andy Hall, [The Dictatorship Eval](https://freesystems.substack.com/p/the-dictatorship-eval) — a "freedom eval"; note the finding that models can't evaluate the systems they serve. **Bridge to the empirical track:** flourishing/autonomy evals are an underexplored project space where the philosophy and mech interp tracks could meet (1)
